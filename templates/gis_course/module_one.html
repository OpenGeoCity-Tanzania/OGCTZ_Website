📚 Module 1: GIS Fundamentals

The Science of Capturing and Representing the Real World in a Computer

🎯 Learning Objectives:

By the end of this module, you will understand:

  • How the Earth's shape is mathematically defined for mapping
  • The difference between geographic and projected coordinate systems
  • How spatial and attribute data work together in GIS
  • Why scale and generalization matter in mapmaking
  • How to represent real-world features digitally using vectors and rasters
🤔 Core Question:

How do we accurately capture the infinite complexity of the real world and store it inside a finite computer?

1Geodesy: The Foundation of Location

Definition: Geodesy is the science of measuring Earth's shape and size. It's the foundation for all mapping because before we can map anything, we need to answer: "What is the Earth?"

🌍 Real-World Scenario:

Imagine you're holding a GPS unit in Dar es Salaam. You get coordinates: -6.8, 39.3. But coordinates from where? On which Earth model? Without geodesy, these numbers would be meaningless.

The Problem: The Earth Isn't a Sphere

The Earth is an irregular shape called a geoid—a bumpy, lumpy ball shaped by mean sea level. It's defined by gravity, not mathematics, making it impossible to use directly for calculations.

Visual Analogy: Imagine an orange. The gravitational bumps and lumps on Earth are like the uneven texture of the orange peel. You can't do math with that.

Solution 1: The Ellipsoid (or Spheroid)

An ellipsoid is a smooth, mathematical surface that approximates the geoid. It's created by rotating an ellipse around the Earth's axis, flattening the poles.

  • What it is: A mathematical shape that is "good enough" but not perfect
  • Visual analogy: Like wrapping a smooth rubber skin around the bumpy orange
  • Advantage: You can do math with it
  • Disadvantage: It's still not perfectly accurate everywhere

Solution 2: The Datum

A datum is a specific version of an ellipsoid that is anchored (fixed) to the Earth at a particular point to provide the best possible fit for a specific region.

Think of it as: A datum is the "starting point" (the 0,0,0) for all coordinates in a region. Choose the wrong datum, and all your coordinates will be systematically wrong.
📍 Example: Why Datum Matters

The same physical location on the ground has DIFFERENT coordinates depending on the datum:

  • WGS84 (GPS standard): -6.8023°, 39.2788° (Dar es Salaam)
  • Arc 1960 (old East African standard): 9,249,753 m, 10,023,456 m (UTM coordinates)

Both point to the same place! But if you use WGS84 coordinates on a map made in Arc 1960, you could be off by 200+ meters.

Key Datums You Need to Know

  • WGS84 (World Geodetic System 1984): The global standard. Used by GPS. Its center is Earth's center of mass. Good for the planet, not the best for any single location.
  • Arc 1960 / Clarke 1880: A common East African datum. Its ellipsoid is oriented to fit the African continent best. Used in older Tanzania maps.
  • Airy 1830: Used in some international mapping
⚠️ Practical Tip: Always check what datum a map uses. If you're combining data from multiple sources, they MUST be in the same datum.

2Coordinate Reference Systems (CRS): The Language of Location

A CRS tells the computer: "These numbers (e.g., -6.8, 39.2) represent a location on Earth, and here's how to interpret them."

Type 1: Geographic Coordinate Systems (GCS)

What: Uses latitude and longitude (spherical coordinates) on a 3D ellipsoid.

  • Units: Degrees (°), minutes ('), seconds (")
  • Accuracy: Spherical—not flat
  • Example: -6.8023°, 39.2788° (Dar es Salaam)
🗺️ Visual Analogy - GCS:

Think of lines of latitude and longitude like a 3D grid wrapped around the Earth. Each intersection is a location. Perfect for "where is it?" but not for "how far is it?"

Type 2: Projected Coordinate Systems (PCS)

What: Flattens the 3D GCS onto a 2D plane using mathematics.

  • Units: Meters, feet, etc. (not degrees)
  • Accuracy: Planar—works well for measuring and area calculation
  • Trade-off: Every projection distorts the world in some way
🍊 Visual Analogy - PCS:

Imagine peeling an orange and trying to flatten the peel onto a table. You HAVE to stretch, shrink, or tear some parts. Map projections do the same with the Earth.

Which One Should You Use?

  • GCS (Lat/Lon): For storing data, working across the planet, or with GPS
  • PCS (UTM, Mercator, etc.): For mapping, measuring distances, calculating areas

Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) for Tanzania

UTM is a worldwide standard that divides Earth into 60 vertical strips (zones). Tanzania spans two zones:

  • UTM Zone 36S: Western Tanzania
  • UTM Zone 37S: Eastern Tanzania (including Dar es Salaam, Zanzibar, Dodoma)
📍 Same Location in Different CRS:

Dodoma University (Tanzania):

  • GCS (WGS84): -6.1667°, 35.7500°
  • PCS (UTM 37S): 632,450 m E, 9,317,600 m N

3Data Models: How We Store the World

A data model is the "digital skin" of the map. How do we represent buildings, rivers, forests, and mountains in a computer?

Vector Data Model: Discrete Objects

Best for: Well-defined, discrete objects with clear boundaries.

  • Points (0D): No area or length. Examples: trees, wells, cities (at small scale), schools
  • Lines (1D): Have length but no area. Examples: rivers, roads, pipelines, power lines
  • Polygons (2D): Have area. Examples: lakes, forest patches, country borders, farmland, buildings
🏘️ Example: Village Representation

Representing a village in vector format:

  • Points: Houses, shops, wells, flagpoles
  • Lines: Roads, footpaths, irrigation channels
  • Polygons: Forest patches, cultivated fields, community center (as an area)

Raster Data Model: Continuous Surfaces

Best for: Continuous phenomena and satellite imagery.

A raster is a grid of equally-sized cells (pixels). Each cell holds a single value:

  • Satellite Imagery: Each pixel = brightness/color value (0-255 for each band)
  • Elevation/DEM: Each pixel = height in meters
  • Temperature Maps: Each pixel = temperature in degrees
  • Rainfall Maps: Each pixel = total rainfall in mm
🛰️ Example: From Vector to Raster

A forest represented as vector (polygon boundary) shows the exact edge. The same forest as raster shows which pixels contain forest trees. Raster is better for analysis (e.g., "What's the total forest area in this region?") because you just count pixels.

4Attribute Data: Giving Life to Shapes

A shape on a map is just geometry until you attach information to it. This is what makes GIS powerful.

The Concept: Spatial + Attribute = Information

Spatial Data: Where is it? (coordinates, geometry)

Attribute Data: What is it? (properties, characteristics)

The Link: A unique ID connects each shape to a row in a table.

💧 Example: A Well Registry

Spatial Part (Map): A point at coordinates (632,422 m E, 9,318,000 m N)

Attribute Part (Table):

Well_ID Type Depth_m Water_Quality
W001 Dug Well 42 Good

The Magic Query: "Show me all wells with Good water quality that are deeper than 40 meters in the northern ward."

Why This Matters

  • Shape alone tells you " WHERE " something is
  • Attributes tell you " WHAT " it is and its properties
  • Together, they let you answer complex questions about the world

5Scale & Generalization: Managing the Detail

Map scale is the ratio between distance on the map and distance on the ground. It dictates how much detail you can show.

Understanding Scale

  • Large Scale (1:1,000): Shows a small area with great detail. You can see individual buildings. Think: City center map
  • Medium Scale (1:50,000): Shows a larger area with moderate detail. Think: District map
  • Small Scale (1:1,000,000): Shows a huge area with minimal detail. You can see countries. Think: Continental map
📊 Visual Example: Dodoma Region at Different Scales
  • 1:5,000: You can see streets, individual buildings, small water sources
  • 1:100,000: You see major roads, towns, large district features
  • 1:1,000,000: You see Tanzania as a whole, just the name "Dodoma"

Generalization: The Art of Simplification

You cannot show every house in Tanzania on a map of Africa. Generalization is the process of simplifying data for display at smaller scales.

  • Selection: Choose only the most important features. (Show major highways, hide dirt roads)
  • Simplification: Reduce detail in lines. (Smooth a coastline, remove small wiggles)
  • Aggregation: Merge small features into larger ones. (Individual houses → "Residential Zone")
  • Displacement: Move features slightly to avoid overlap at small scale
Key Insight: Different scales serve different purposes. A farmer needs a 1:5,000 map to plan irrigation channels. A transportation minister needs a 1:500,000 map to plan highways. Both are correct—they just answer different questions.

📖 Summary for Revision

  • Geodesy: We use a datum (like WGS84 or Arc 1960) to anchor an ellipsoid to Earth for accurate measurement
  • CRS: We store in GCS (lat/lon), display/measure in PCS (UTM) for correct distances in meters
  • Data Models: Vector (points/lines/polygons) for discrete objects; Raster (grid) for continuous surfaces
  • Attributes: Spatial geometry + attribute table = complete geographic information
  • Scale: Map scale controls detail level. Generalization simplifies data for smaller scales
